# 01 — Player Flow And Spatial Readability

Players do not experience a level as a floor plan. They experience it as a sequence of decisions under time and pressure.

## Start With The Player Question

At any point in a space, the player should be able to answer:

- where can I go?
- what looks important?
- what looks dangerous?
- where can I take cover or recover?

If the layout does not answer those questions quickly, the level is already weaker than it looks.

## Routes Need Distinct Jobs

Multiple paths are only valuable when they create distinct gameplay meaning.

Common route roles:

- critical path
- flank route
- shortcut
- risk/reward detour
- retreat line
- reveal corridor

Do not add branches just to make the map feel bigger. Add them when they change decisions.

## Landmarks Prevent Spatial Drift

Players orient by memorable anchors:

- a statue
- a tower
- a bright doorway
- a fire pit
- a skyline break
- a unique silhouette

Without landmarks, large spaces flatten into “more floor.”

## Sightlines Are Gameplay Tools

Sightlines control:

- anticipation
- target acquisition
- fear
- navigation confidence
- reveal timing

Long sightlines can create pressure or orientation. Broken sightlines can create safety, mystery, or ambush space.

Use them intentionally instead of letting geometry accidentally decide them.

## Cover Should Exist At Different Ranges

Combat spaces are easier to read when cover exists at multiple engagement bands:

- near cover for immediate survival
- mid-range cover for trading space
- far cover or hard boundaries for long-range decisions

If all cover sits at one distance or one height, the encounter flattens.

## Pacing Lives In Transitions

Good levels change tempo through:

- chokepoints
- openings
- height shifts
- compression and release
- safe rooms before stress spikes
- strong reveals after quiet setup

A level that stays at one intensity becomes forgettable even if the geometry is technically fine.

## Blockout Is About Truth

Blockout exists to answer:

- is the space readable?
- is movement satisfying?
- are entrances and exits fair?
- does traversal time feel right?
- does the encounter support the intended behavior?

It is not an excuse to start decorating early.

## Verticality Must Clarify, Not Confuse

Height changes are useful when they create:

- tactical advantage
- route variety
- landmark value
- pacing contrast

They are harmful when they create navigation ambiguity or camera problems with no gameplay upside.

## Critical Path Should Be Legible Without Neon Arrows

The player should usually find the intended route through:

- geometry framing
- lighting emphasis
- landmark pull
- enemy placement
- sound cues
- door scale and silhouette

If the only way to teach the path is a giant marker, the space is not carrying its share of the design.

## Playable Space Beats Decorative Mass

A smaller space with clear decisions is stronger than a huge one with no tactical meaning.

Do not equate “large” with “good.”

## Key Takeaway

At every level-design step ask:

- what is the player deciding here?
- what teaches them that?
- what keeps them oriented?
- where do they feel pressure, safety, and release?

That is the actual subject of level design. The geometry is only the medium.
