# 01 — How Nature Works

You are building virtual worlds. To make them feel real, you must understand how real ecosystems work. This isn't artistic preference — it's science. Nature follows rules, and players instinctively notice when those rules are broken.

## Vegetation Layers

A natural forest is NOT "trees on flat ground." It's a vertical stack of 5-6 distinct layers, each with different plants competing for light:

### 1. Canopy (15-40m)
- The tallest trees forming the roof of the forest
- Species: Oak, Beech, Pine, Fir, Maple (depends on biome)
- Spacing: 5-15m apart (they compete for light)
- NOT uniform — gaps where old trees fell, denser where soil is rich
- Crowns spread wide to catch sunlight, creating shade below

### 2. Understory (5-15m)
- Shorter trees that tolerate shade
- Species: Dogwood, Holly, young versions of canopy trees
- Exists only where canopy has gaps that let some light through
- Denser at forest edges where light enters sideways

### 3. Shrub Layer (1-5m)
- Bushes, large ferns, brambles
- Species: Hazel, Elder, Rhododendron, large ferns
- Densest along paths, clearings, and streams where light reaches
- SPARSE under dense canopy (too dark)

### 4. Herb/Ground Cover (10cm-1m)
- Wildflowers, grass, small ferns, mosses growing thick
- Species: Bluebells, foxgloves, wild garlic, sword ferns
- SEASONAL — different species bloom at different times
- Thickest in clearings and at forest edges

### 5. Ground/Forest Floor (0-10cm)
- Fallen leaves, decomposing wood, mushrooms, moss, lichen
- NOT bare soil — always covered with organic matter
- Moss grows on north-facing surfaces (in northern hemisphere)
- Fallen logs and branches are natural, not "debris to clean up"

### 6. Root/Soil Layer (underground)
- Not visible but affects what grows where
- Rocky soil = shallow-rooted trees, more rock outcrops visible
- Deep rich soil = tall dense forest
- Sandy soil = pine forests, heather

## Terrain Drives Everything

**Slope determines vegetation:**
- 0-15 degrees (flat) — Grass, meadows, dense forest possible
- 15-35 degrees (moderate slope) — Trees can grow but less dense, more exposed rock
- 35-55 degrees (steep) — Only rock, sparse hardy plants, no trees
- 55+ degrees (cliff) — Bare rock, maybe lichens

**Height determines biome:**
- Valley floor — Lush, dense, often near water
- Mid-slope — Standard forest
- Upper slopes — Thinner soil, shorter trees, more wind-shaped
- Treeline (~2000-3000m depending on latitude) — No more trees above this
- Alpine — Grass, moss, rock only
- Snow line — Permanent snow/ice

**Water determines density:**
- Near rivers/lakes — Lusher vegetation, willows, reeds, ferns
- Dry ridges — Sparser, hardier species
- Bogs/wetlands — Specialized plants, no trees, moss-dominated
- Rain shadow (behind mountains) — Drier, different species

## Biome Types

### Temperate Deciduous Forest (Europe, Eastern US)
- Oak, Beech, Maple, Birch
- Seasonal: green summer, colored autumn, bare winter
- Rich understory with ferns and wildflowers
- Moderate rainfall, distinct seasons
- Color palette: deep greens, browns, seasonal yellows/oranges/reds

### Boreal/Taiga (Scandinavia, Canada, Russia)
- Pine, Spruce, Fir — mostly evergreen
- Sparse understory (too dark under conifers)
- Ground cover: moss, lichen, blueberry bushes
- Cold, long winters
- Color palette: dark greens, grey-greens, white snow

### Mediterranean (Southern Europe, California)
- Olive, Cork Oak, Pine — drought-adapted
- Sparse canopy, lots of sunlight reaching ground
- Shrub-dominated (maquis/chaparral)
- Dry summers, mild wet winters
- Color palette: silver-greens, dry yellows, terracotta

### Tropical Rainforest
- Dense multi-layer canopy, almost no light at ground level
- Epiphytes (plants growing on other plants), vines, lianas
- Very sparse ground cover (too dark)
- Year-round warmth and rain
- Color palette: intense greens, dark shadows, bright flowers

### Grassland/Prairie
- Almost no trees (too dry or soil too thin)
- Grass dominates, 30cm to 2m tall
- Seasonal flowering
- Wind-swept, exposed
- Color palette: golden yellows, dried browns, seasonal greens

## Distribution Rules

Nature is NEVER uniform. Real vegetation follows these patterns:

### Clustering
- Trees of the same species grow in clusters (seeds fall near parent)
- Young trees grow near old ones (shade, soil quality)
- Mushrooms cluster around dead wood
- Flowers cluster where light patches exist

### Edge Effects
- Forest edges are DENSER than interiors (more light = more growth)
- The transition from meadow to forest is gradual (grass -> shrubs -> small trees -> forest)
- Riverbanks have distinct vegetation (reeds, willows, moss)
- Path edges have trampled grass transitioning to untouched growth

### Randomness Within Rules
- No two trees are the same height, angle, or spacing
- Scale variation: 0.7x to 1.3x for same species
- Rotation: fully random yaw, slight random lean
- Color variation: even same species varies (soil, sun exposure, age)
- Dead/damaged specimens mixed in (5-15% of trees have dead branches, lean, or damage)

### Absence is Information
- Bare patches tell a story (too rocky, too wet, recently disturbed, path)
- Clearings exist where trees fell, fire burned, or animals graze
- Steep rock faces are deliberately bare
- The ABSENCE of vegetation is as important as its presence

## Light and Atmosphere

### How Sunlight Works in a Forest
- Direct light penetrates through canopy gaps creating "god rays"
- Most forest floor is in dappled shade (moving light patches)
- East-facing slopes get morning sun (warmer colors), west-facing get evening sun
- North-facing slopes (northern hemisphere) are cooler, moister, more moss

### Atmospheric Depth
- Distant objects become bluer and hazier (atmospheric scattering)
- Morning = fog in valleys, clear on ridges
- After rain = heightened color saturation, mist rising from warm ground
- Golden hour = warm orange light, long shadows, dramatic contrast

### Color Temperature Varies
- Open sky areas = cool blue ambient light (6500K+)
- Under canopy = filtered green-tinted light
- Sunset/sunrise = warm (3000-4000K)
- Overcast = neutral diffused light (5500K), very soft shadows

## Key Takeaway

When you build an environment, ask yourself at every step:
- **WHY** would this plant grow HERE? (soil, light, water, slope)
- **WHAT** would be next to it? (companion species, natural groupings)
- **WHERE** would it NOT grow? (too steep, too dry, too shaded, path)
- **HOW** would it look? (variation in age, health, size, color)

If you can't answer these questions, you don't understand the environment well enough to build it.
